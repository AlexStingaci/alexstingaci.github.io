Bernoulli Process & Security Random‑Walk — A Narrative Bridge

From empirical frequencies to cumulative scores: why Homework 6 and 7 are telling the same story in two voices.

$q=1-(1-p)^m$ $B_n=\sum_{i=1}^n Y_i\sim\mathrm{Bin}(n,q)$ $S_n=n-2B_n$ $\mathbb E[S_n]=n(1-2q),\ \mathrm Var(S_n)=4nq(1-q)$
Reading guide. We will walk through the logic in prose, minimizing bulleted checklists. Equations appear when they clarify the narrative, but the goal is intuition first, formalism second.

1) One story, two cameras

Imagine a long diary of weekly outcomes. Each week either a breach happens ($Y_i=1$) or it does not ($Y_i=0$). If every week the chance of breach is the same, $q$, and weeks are independent, then the total number of breached weeks up to $n$ is $B_n=\sum_{i=1}^n Y_i$, a binomial random variable.

Homework 6 watched this diary through the lens of proportions, plotting the empirical frequency $\hat q_n=B_n/n$ and showing—via the Law of Large Numbers—that it stabilizes near $q$. Homework 7 used a different camera: it turned each week into a step, $X_i=+1$ for a secure week and $X_i=-1$ for a breach, and followed the cumulative score $S_n=\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ as a biased random walk.

The surprise is that nothing deep separates these views. They are algebraically intertwined by the identity $$S_n = n - 2B_n,\qquad \hat q_n = \frac{B_n}{n} = \frac{1}{2}\left(1 - \frac{S_n}{n}\right).$$ Thus, the LLN statement “$\hat q_n\to q$” is the same as “$S_n/n\to 1-2q$”. Changing cameras changes the picture, but not the plot.

2) What Pascal already knew

Counting how many diaries end with exactly $b$ breaches is a classical exercise: choose the $b$ weeks among $n$ positions, hence $\binom{n}{b}$. In the random‑walk language, this is the number of paths that finish at score $s=n-2b$. The coefficients $\binom{n}{b}$ are the familiar entries in row $n$ of Pascal’s triangle.

Pascal’s rule, $$\binom{n}{b}=\binom{n-1}{b}+\binom{n-1}{b-1},$$ has a vivid interpretation here. Arriving at $(n,b)$ either you were secure in the last week (so you stayed with $b$ breaches and came from $(n-1,b)$) or you were breached (so you increased to $b$ and came from $(n-1,b-1)$). The same “last‑step” decomposition produces a one‑line recurrence for the distribution of the random walk: $$\mathbb P(S_n=s)=(1-q)\,\mathbb P(S_{n-1}=s-1)+q\,\mathbb P(S_{n-1}=s+1).$$ In short, Pascal’s triangle is not just arithmetic; it is the walk’s combinatorial shadow.

In the symmetric case $q=\tfrac12$, the triangle’s central coefficients dominate. This is why, when the walk has zero drift, most mass stacks around $S_n\approx 0$ and the peak probability at $S_{2m}=0$ is $\binom{2m}{m}2^{-2m}$.

3) The binomial expansion as a machine

There is another way to see the same structure, this time through generating functions. If we encode the random walk by powers of $z$, the score after $n$ weeks has generating function $$G_n(z)=\mathbb E[z^{S_n}]=\big((1-q)z+q z^{-1}\big)^n.$$ Expanding with the binomial theorem makes the coefficients of $z^s$ fall out automatically; reading those coefficients is tantamount to reading the distribution of $S_n$.

Moment calculations inherit the same convenience. The moment generating function $$M_{S_n}(t)=\big((1-q)e^{t}+q e^{-t}\big)^n$$ yields the drift $\mathbb E[S_n]=n(1-2q)$ and the variance $\mathrm Var(S_n)=4nq(1-q)$ by differentiation. With proper centering and scaling, the Central Limit Theorem says that the standardized $S_n$ looks Gaussian for large $n$—a mirror image of the CLT for $\hat q_n$.

4) A Fibonacci detour

Fibonacci numbers seem far from security logs, but they appear the moment we impose a simple policy constraint: no two breaches in a row. If we only count binary diaries with no adjacent 1’s, the number of such strings of length $n$ is $F_{n+2}$. This identity is a consequence of the “shallow diagonal” sums of Pascal’s triangle, for example $$F_n=\sum_{k\ge 0}\binom{n-k-1}{k}.$$

Turning the count into a probability is immediate under Bernoulli($q$): sum the appropriate binomial terms over the admissible patterns. The resulting expression, $$\sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor n/2\rfloor}\binom{n-k+1}{k}\,q^{k}(1-q)^{n-k},$$ quantifies “cool‑down” policies by telling us how likely it is that reality (with breach rate $q$) accidentally complies with “no consecutive breaches” over $n$ weeks.

5) Where the two views diverge in practice

Although they share a backbone, the two homeworks emphasize different narratives. The LLN perspective treats the process as a proportion that stabilizes; its plots live on the interval $[0,1]$ and speak the language of frequencies. The random‑walk view turns the same evidence into motion: scores live on a lattice $\{n,n-2,\dots,-n\}$, and the key visual is a cloud of trajectories with an evident drift.

In operational terms, the drift $1-2q$ is an interpretable “security advantage per week.” When $q<\tfrac12$, the walk tends to climb; when $q>\tfrac12$, it tends to sink. The special case $q=\tfrac12$ is a fair walk with no average trend, dominated by the central binomial coefficients.

Finally, the security scenario adds one layer that LLN by itself does not mention: multiple attackers. If each attacker breaches with probability $p$ and there are $m$ of them, the weekly breach probability becomes $$q=1-(1-p)^m\approx 1-e^{-mp}\quad\text{for small }p.$$ All the structure above—Pascal, binomial expansion, CLT—remains intact after this substitution.

6) Closing picture

Homework 6 and Homework 7 are not different models but different projections of the same Bernoulli world. One projects onto the axis of frequencies ($\hat q_n$), the other onto the axis of cumulative scores ($S_n$). Both are governed by binomial coefficients, both inherit Pascal’s recursion, both expand neatly with the binomial theorem, and both submit to the LLN and the CLT.

If you keep a single mental blueprint: “Choose-your-weeks” explains the counts ($\binom{n}{b}$), “last step” explains the recurrences, and “binomial expansion” explains the probabilities and moments. Everything else—Fibonacci detours, Catalan constraints, symmetric peaks—are natural corollaries along that path.
